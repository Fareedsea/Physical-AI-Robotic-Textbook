# Glossary

This glossary provides definitions for key terms used throughout the Physical AI & Humanoid Robotics Textbook.

## A

**Actuator**: A component of a robot that converts control signals into physical motion or force.

**AI (Artificial Intelligence)**: The simulation of human intelligence processes by machines, especially computer systems.

**Anthropomorphic**: Having human-like characteristics or form.

**Autonomous System**: A system that can operate without continuous human control.

## B

**Bipedal Locomotion**: Walking on two legs, like humans.

**Behavioral Cloning**: A machine learning technique where a system learns to perform tasks by mimicking expert demonstrations.

**Biomechanics**: The study of mechanical laws relating to the movement or structure of living organisms.

## C

**Computer Vision**: A field of artificial intelligence that trains computers to interpret and understand the visual world.

**Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)**: A class of deep neural networks, most commonly applied to analyzing visual imagery.

**Control System**: A device or set of devices that manages, commands, directs, or regulates the behavior of other parts of a system.

**Cobot (Collaborative Robot)**: A robot designed to work safely alongside humans in a shared workspace.

## D

**Deep Learning**: A subset of machine learning that uses neural networks with multiple layers to model complex patterns in data.

**Degrees of Freedom (DOF)**: The number of independent movements a robot or mechanical system can perform.

**Dexterous Manipulation**: The ability to perform fine, precise manipulation tasks with robot hands.

## E

**Embodied Intelligence**: Intelligence that emerges from the interaction between an agent and its environment, where the body's form and capabilities shape cognitive processes.

**Encoder**: A sensor that measures the position or speed of rotation of a shaft or axle.

**End-Effector**: The device at the end of a robotic arm that interacts with the environment, such as a gripper or tool.

**Exteroceptive Sensors**: Sensors that measure external environmental properties.

**Explainable AI (XAI)**: Artificial intelligence that humans can understand and trust.

## F

**Feedback Control**: A control system that uses feedback to adjust commands based on actual performance.

**Forward Kinematics**: Determining the end-effector position from joint angles.

**Force Control**: Controlling the forces applied by a robot during interaction with the environment.

## G

**Gaussian Mixture Model**: A probabilistic model that assumes all the data points are generated from a mixture of several Gaussian distributions.

**Gripper**: A device at the end of a robotic arm designed to grasp and hold objects.

**Grounded Cognition**: The idea that cognitive processes are shaped by the physical experiences of an embodied agent.

## H

**Haptic Feedback**: The use of touch sensation to communicate with a user.

**Human-Robot Interaction (HRI)**: The study of interactions between humans and robots.

**Humanoid Robot**: A robot designed to resemble the human body structure, typically having a head, torso, two arms, and two legs.

## I

**Imitation Learning**: Learning to perform tasks by observing and mimicking expert demonstrations.

**Inverse Kinematics**: Determining joint angles to achieve a desired end-effector position.

**Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU)**: An electronic device that measures and reports a body's specific force, angular rate, and sometimes the magnetic field surrounding the body.

**Intention Recognition**: The ability to identify and understand human intentions during interaction.

## K

**Kinematics**: The branch of mechanics concerned with the motion of objects without reference to the forces that cause the motion.

**Kalman Filter**: An algorithm that uses a series of measurements observed over time to estimate unknown variables.

## L

**LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging)**: A remote sensing method that uses light in the form of a pulsed laser to measure distances.

**Locomotion**: The ability to move from one place to another.

**Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)**: A type of recurrent neural network architecture that can learn long-term dependencies.

## M

**Machine Learning**: A type of artificial intelligence that enables computers to learn and make decisions from data without being explicitly programmed.

**Manipulation**: The ability to handle or control objects in the environment.

**Mobile Robot**: A robot that can move within its environment using wheels, tracks, legs, or other locomotion mechanisms.

**Multimodal Interaction**: Interaction that uses multiple communication channels such as visual, auditory, and haptic.

## N

**Neural Network**: A series of algorithms that endeavors to recognize underlying relationships in a set of data through a process that mimics the way the human brain operates.

**Nonholonomic System**: A system whose state depends on the path taken to achieve it, such as a car that cannot move sideways.

## O

**Obstacle Avoidance**: The ability of a robot to detect and avoid obstacles in its path.

**Open-Loop Control**: A control system that operates without feedback about the actual performance.

**Operational Space Control**: A control framework that allows robots to control task-relevant variables directly in the operational space.

## P

**Perception**: The ability of a robot to interpret and understand its environment through sensory data.

**PID Controller**: A control loop mechanism employing proportional, integral, and derivative terms to minimize error.

**Point Cloud**: A set of data points in space, representing the external surface of an object or environment.

**Proprioceptive Sensors**: Sensors that measure internal robot states such as joint angles and forces.

## R

**Reinforcement Learning**: A type of machine learning where an agent learns to make decisions by performing actions and receiving rewards or penalties.

**Robotics**: An interdisciplinary branch of engineering and science that includes mechanical engineering, electrical engineering, computer science, and others.

**ROS (Robot Operating System)**: A flexible framework for writing robot software that provides a collection of tools, libraries, and conventions.

**Rigid Body Dynamics**: The study of the motion of rigid bodies under the influence of forces and torques.

## S

**Sensor Fusion**: The process of combining sensory data or data derived from disparate sources to achieve improved accuracy and reliability.

**SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping)**: The computational problem of constructing or updating a map of an unknown environment while simultaneously keeping track of an agent's location.

**Social Robot**: A robot designed to interact with humans in a natural and socially acceptable manner.

**State Estimation**: The process of determining the state of a system from noisy measurements.

**Supervised Learning**: A type of machine learning where the model is trained on labeled data.

## T

**Tactile Sensors**: Sensors that detect touch, pressure, or other physical contact.

**Teleoperation**: The remote operation of a robot by a human operator.

**Time-of-Flight Camera**: A camera that measures the time it takes for light to travel to an object and back to determine distance.

**Trajectory Planning**: The process of determining the path a robot should follow over time.

## U

**Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV)**: An aircraft without a human pilot aboard, also known as a drone.

**Unsupervised Learning**: A type of machine learning that learns patterns from unlabeled data.

**Ultrasonic Sensor**: A sensor that uses sound waves above the range of human hearing to measure distances.

## V

**Visual Servoing**: Using visual feedback for robot control, where the robot's actions are controlled based on visual information.

**VSLAM (Visual SLAM)**: SLAM using visual sensors such as cameras.

## W

**Whole-Body Control**: A control approach that coordinates all the degrees of freedom of a robot simultaneously to achieve multiple tasks.

**Wheeled Robot**: A robot that uses wheels for locomotion.

## Z

**Zero Moment Point (ZMP)**: A criterion used in robotics to maintain balance during locomotion, representing the point where the total of the moments of the active forces equals zero.
